Linalool Cosmetic Regulation

INCI: Linalool · CAS: 78-70-6 · Updated April 2026

Linalool is one of the most common fragrance ingredients in cosmetics. It occurs naturally in lavender, mint, cinnamon, and hundreds of other plants. If a product has a scent, there's a good chance Linalool is in it.

And yet, almost no country specifically regulates it — except the EU.

Why the EU Is Different

The EU classifies Linalool as a fragrance allergen. Under the EU Cosmetics Regulation (EC 1223/2009), Annex III requires that certain fragrance substances be individually listed on the product label when they exceed specific concentration thresholds. The thresholds are extremely low: 0.001% for leave-on products (10 parts per million) and 0.01% for rinse-off products.

This means that virtually any fragranced product sold in the EU needs to declare Linalool on its ingredient list, because it's nearly impossible to create a scented product that stays below 10 ppm.

Practical Impact

For brands exporting to the EU, this creates an extra compliance step that doesn't exist in other markets. The product formula doesn't need to change — Linalool isn't banned or concentration-limited. But the label does need to change. And if you're shipping a product with a generic "Fragrance/Parfum" ingredient listing, it won't pass EU compliance unless the individual allergens including Linalool are declared.

This is the kind of regulatory difference that holds shipments at customs. The product is perfectly fine chemically — it's a labeling issue that nobody sees coming until it's too late.

Other EU Fragrance Allergens

Linalool isn't the only fragrance ingredient with EU labeling requirements. The regulation covers 80+ fragrance allergens (expanded from 26 in 2023) including Limonene, Citronellol, Geraniol, Eugenol, and Coumarin. If your product contains any fragrance at all, you likely need to declare multiple allergens on the EU label.
