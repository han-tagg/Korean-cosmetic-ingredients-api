Country Highlights

🇰🇷 Korea — The Base Dataset

Korea is the foundation of this API. The KCIA dictionary contains 21,796 cosmetic ingredients with Korean names, INCI names, CAS numbers, purposes, and MFDS regulatory classifications. Every ingredient has a regulation_status field: Prohibited (172+), Restricted (1,046+), or Not Listed (20,000+). For restricted ingredients, Korean-specific concentration limits and product-type conditions are provided in full.

🇪🇺 EU — The Most Comprehensive Regulation

The EU Cosmetics Regulation (EC 1223/2009) is the most detailed regulatory framework in the database. It includes Annex II (1,700+ prohibited), Annex III (300+ restricted with conditions), plus allergen labeling requirements for 80+ fragrance allergens (expanded from 26 in 2023). EU data includes product-type restrictions, maximum concentrations, age restrictions, and required warning labels. See Korea vs EU comparison.

🇨🇳 China — Growing Regulation

China's NMPA has been rapidly expanding its cosmetic ingredient regulations, increasingly aligning with international standards while maintaining its own prohibited and restricted substance lists. Chinese data includes concentration limits and product-type restrictions, with both the Chinese original and English translations.

🇯🇵 Japan — Unique Concentration System

Japan uses a distinctive regulation approach: limits are expressed as "grams per 100g of product" rather than percentages. Japan's limits can be surprisingly different — Salicylic Acid is limited to 0.2% in Japan while most countries allow 2-3%. Japan also has no specific age restrictions for many ingredients that other countries restrict for children.

🌏 ASEAN — 10 Countries, Mostly Aligned

The ASEAN Cosmetic Directive creates a common framework for 10 Southeast Asian nations. Regulation is largely aligned with the EU but with some national exceptions — for example, Indonesia has separate rules for certain preservatives. The API captures both the common ASEAN rules and noted exceptions.

🇨🇦 Canada — Unique Purity Requirements

Canada's Cosmetic Ingredient Hotlist includes concentration limits and unique requirements not found elsewhere. For example, Glycerin requires pharmaceutical-grade purity for diethylene glycol impurities in oral and leave-on products — a manufacturing quality control requirement unique to Canada. Triclosan has dioxin impurity testing requirements.

🇧🇷 Brazil & 🇦🇷 Argentina — Following the EU Lead

Brazil (ANVISA) and Argentina (ANMAT) tend to align with EU regulatory decisions, often with similar concentration limits and prohibited substance lists. When the EU reduced Propylparaben limits from 0.4% to 0.14%, both countries followed suit. When the EU banned Isobutylparaben, both countries did the same.

🇹🇼 Taiwan — The "Strictest Standard" Approach

Taiwan's TFDA has a unique regulatory philosophy: when EU, US, and Japan standards differ, Taiwan adopts the strictest among them. This is explicitly stated in their regulations. For preservatives, if the EU limit is 0.14% and Japan's is 0.2%, Taiwan will apply 0.14%.
