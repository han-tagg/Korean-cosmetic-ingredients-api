Paraben Cosmetic Regulations by Country

22 paraben variants tracked · Data from 10 countries · Updated April 2026

Parabens are the most widely used preservatives in cosmetics. They prevent bacterial growth and extend shelf life. But not all parabens are treated equally — regulators around the world have drawn very different lines on which ones are safe and at what concentration.

The Three Tiers of Paraben Regulation

Paraben regulations break down into three distinct tiers depending on the chain length of the molecule:

Methylparaben is the one paraben everyone agrees on. Short chain, well-studied, low endocrine concern. The concentration limits are nearly identical worldwide.

In 2014, the EU dropped Propylparaben's limit from 0.4% to 0.14% — nearly three times stricter — after the SCCS flagged endocrine disruption concerns. Brazil and Argentina followed. Korea, China, Japan, and ASEAN kept the original 0.4% limit. Same safety data, different conclusions.

This is the starkest regulatory divide in cosmetics. Five major markets banned Isobutylparaben entirely. The EU's rationale wasn't that they proved it dangerous — it was that there wasn't enough safety data to confirm it was safe. A precautionary ban. Korea and Japan reviewed similar evidence and decided concentration limits were sufficient.

The practical consequence: a product containing Isobutylparaben that's legally sold in Korea would be illegal in the EU, China, Brazil, Argentina, ASEAN, and now California.

Also Banned: Isopropylparaben, Phenylparaben, Benzylparaben, Pentylparaben

Isobutylparaben isn't alone. The EU also banned Isopropylparaben (CAS 4191-73-5), Phenylparaben (CAS 17696-62-7), Benzylparaben (CAS 94-18-8), and Pentylparaben (CAS 6521-29-5) under Annex II. All five were removed due to insufficient safety data, not because harm was conclusively demonstrated.

Korean Regulation Detail

Korea regulates parabens as a group under the name "p-하이드록시벤조익애씨드, 그 염류 및 에스텔류" (p-Hydroxybenzoic acid, its salts and esters). The 0.4% single / 0.8% mixed limit applies to the entire group, with Phenylparaben specifically excluded (prohibited). For Isobutylparaben, Propylparaben, and Butylparaben, there's an additional warning: products for children under 3 must not be applied to the diaper area.
