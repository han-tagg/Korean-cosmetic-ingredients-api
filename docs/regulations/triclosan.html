Triclosan Cosmetic Regulation by Country

INCI: Triclosan · CAS: 3380-34-5 · Data from 9 countries · Updated April 2026

Triclosan is an antibacterial agent that's been used in cosmetics for decades. It's now being phased out worldwide, but every country is doing it at a different speed and with different exceptions. There's no global consensus — just a patchwork of product-specific rules.

What Makes Triclosan Regulation Unique

Unlike most cosmetic ingredients where the debate is about concentration limits, Triclosan regulation is primarily about which product types are allowed. The concentration is almost universally 0.3%, but the list of permitted products gets shorter every year.

Korea's approach is particularly strict — Triclosan is only allowed in rinse-off body cleansers, non-spray deodorants, face powder, and blemish concealer. Leave-on products, sprays, and everything else: banned.

Canada stands out with the lowest mouthwash limit (0.03%) and unique requirements for testing dioxin impurity levels in the raw material — a manufacturing quality control requirement that no other country imposes.

The US Approach

The FDA banned Triclosan in OTC consumer antiseptic wash products in 2017 but hasn't banned it from all cosmetics. This creates an unusual situation where Triclosan is prohibited in hand soap but potentially allowed in other cosmetic products under different regulatory categories.
